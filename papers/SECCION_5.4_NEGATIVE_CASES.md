## Why Some Vague Language Resists Mutation

---

### 5.4 Negative Cases: Why Some Vague Language Resists Mutation

The preceding analysis demonstrates that vague constitutional language—such as Argentina's "bienestar general" (general welfare)—creates vulnerability to doctrinal mutation, enabling transformation of temporary emergency powers into permanent State authority. Argentina's income tax, authorized "provisionally" in 1932 under emergency powers, persists 93 years later with no sunset clause. The fitness landscape (Section IV) showed that vague language + weak institutional checks = high mutation probability.

However, not all countries with vague constitutional emergency language experience permanent mutation. Switzerland and Canada present **negative cases**: despite constitutional provisions allowing broad emergency powers expressed in general terms, neither country has experienced persistent expansion comparable to Argentina. These cases allow us to identify **institutional factors that prevent mutation** even when linguistic vagueness exists.

---

#### Switzerland: Disciplined Emergency Powers

**Constitutional Framework**: Article 185 of the Swiss Federal Constitution (1999, successor to 1874 Constitution Article 102.8-10) grants the Federal Council (executive) authority to "take measures to safeguard external security, independence and neutrality" and "to safeguard internal security" when "serious disturbances of public order or internal or external threats" exist. This language is notably vague: "serious disturbances" and "threats" are undefined, and "measures" are unspecified, creating discretion comparable to Argentina's "bienestar general."

**Empirical Pattern**: Despite this vagueness, Swiss emergency powers have been invoked **only 10 times in 110 years** (1914-2024):
1. **World War I** (1914-1918): Mobilization and economic controls
2. **General Strike** (1918): Brief deployment to restore order
3. **World War II** (1939-1945): Full emergency regime (*Vollmachtenregime*)
4. **Hungarian Refugee Crisis** (1956): Border control measures (6 months)
5. **Cold War nuclear preparedness** (1963-1970): Civil defense (non-invasive)
6. **Oil Crisis** (1973-1974): Energy rationing (8 months)
7. **Chernobyl fallout** (1986): Food safety measures (3 months)
8. **Kosovo refugee crisis** (1999): Expedited asylum processing (4 months)
9. **COVID-19 pandemic** (2020-2022): Public health measures (18 months, with parliamentary renewals)
10. **Ukraine war refugees** (2022-2023): Expedited asylum for Ukrainians (12 months)

Critically, **none of these emergency measures became permanent**. The World War II *Vollmachtenregime*, the most expansive use of emergency powers in Swiss history, was **completely dismantled by 1952**, with all emergency regulations either repealed or converted to ordinary legislation through democratic process. Compare this to Argentina, where 1933 emergency income tax remains in force 91 years later.

**Explanatory Factors**:

**1. Strong Federalism**: Switzerland's cantonal system creates powerful veto players. Emergency measures affecting cantonal competencies (education, police, direct taxation) require cantonal consent or face political/legal challenge. Federal Council cannot unilaterally expand into cantonal domains. Contrast Argentina's weak federalism: provinces lack fiscal autonomy (depend on federal transfers) and cannot effectively resist federal expansion.

**2. Direct Democracy**: Swiss Constitution Article 140 requires mandatory referendum for any federal law deemed "urgent" (*dringlich*) that remains in force beyond one year. Citizens can force referendum on emergency measures, and if rejected, the measure expires immediately. COVID-19 measures were subjected to **three national referendums** (2021-2022), with two confirming and one modifying emergency laws. This creates built-in sunset pressure: emergency measures must convince citizens annually or lapse. Argentina has no national referendum mechanism (except for constitutional reform), eliminating popular brake on executive.

**3. Culture of Consensus (*Konkordanzdemokratie*)**: Swiss political culture emphasizes grand coalition government and proportional representation across linguistic/religious/ideological groups. Emergency powers require consensus across Federal Council (multi-party executive), both houses of Parliament (Council of States and National Council), and cantonal governments. This creates **procedural friction**: invoking emergency is costly politically, discouraging routine use. Argentina's presidential system concentrates power, reducing friction.

**4. Active Judicial Review**: Although Swiss Federal Tribunal cannot review federal legislation for constitutionality (Article 190), it **actively reviews proportionality** of executive emergency measures, requiring: (a) legitimate aim, (b) suitability, (c) necessity, (d) proportionality stricto sensu (balancing). This restrains executive discretion. In *Förderprogramm* (2005), Federal Tribunal struck down economic support measures as exceeding emergency scope. Argentina's judiciary, especially during "mayoría automática" (1990-2003), exhibited minimal review of emergency powers.

**Result**: Switzerland demonstrates that **vague language + institutional friction = low mutation rate**. The same linguistic vagueness that enabled Argentina's permanent income tax coexists in Switzerland with disciplined, temporary emergency use. Difference lies not in text but in **institutional checks** that impose costs on mutation.

#### Canada: The Emergencies Act (1988)

**Constitutional Framework**: The Canadian *Emergencies Act* (1988) replaced the notorious *War Measures Act*, which had been invoked during World War I, World War II, and the 1970 October Crisis (FLQ kidnappings). The 1970 invocation was widely criticized for rights violations (450+ arrests without warrant, military deployment in Quebec), prompting calls for reform.

The 1988 Act distinguishes four emergency types:
1. **Public Welfare Emergency**: natural disaster, disease, accident
2. **Public Order Emergency**: threats to security of Canada (terrorism, espionage)
3. **International Emergency**: war, invasion
4. **War Emergency**: armed conflict involving Canada

For each type, the Act defines thresholds (e.g., "national emergency" = situation "beyond the capacity or authority of a province") and procedures. Critically, all emergencies have **automatic sunset**: declarations expire after **30 days for welfare/order emergencies, 60 days for international, 120 days for war**, unless Parliament votes to extend (requiring debate and majority vote).

**Empirical Pattern**: In 37 years (1988-2025), the *Emergencies Act* has been invoked **only twice**:
1. **COVID-19 pandemic** (2020): **Not invoked**—despite massive public health crisis, federal government relied on ordinary legislation and provincial emergency powers, avoiding federal *Emergencies Act*
2. **Freedom Convoy protests** (February 2022): Invoked for 9 days to clear Ottawa blockades, then **voluntarily revoked** by Prime Minister Trudeau before parliamentary review completed

The 2022 invocation is especially instructive. Despite authority to maintain emergency for 30 days (renewable), Trudeau revoked it after 9 days once blockades cleared, stating "the situation is no longer an emergency." A **mandatory public inquiry** (Rouleau Commission, 2022-2023) examined whether invocation met statutory threshold. Commission concluded invocation was justified but "should be used sparingly" and recommended tighter procedural safeguards. Contrast this to Argentina, where emergency powers persist indefinitely with no ex post accountability.

**Explanatory Factors**:

**1. Automatic Sunset Clause**: The 30-day expiration (30/60/120 depending on type) with mandatory parliamentary renewal every 30 days creates **default termination**. Executive must repeatedly justify continuation; if Parliament refuses renewal, emergency ends. Argentina's emergency laws have no sunset: once enacted, they persist until repealed (which requires overcoming legislative inertia and executive veto).

**2. Judicial Review Under Charter**: Canadian courts apply *Charter of Rights and Freedoms* (1982) even during emergencies. Section 1 allows "reasonable limits" on rights "demonstrably justified in a free and democratic society," but government bears burden of proof. In potential *Emergencies Act* litigation (avoided in 2022 by quick revocation), courts would apply *Oakes* test (proportionality review), likely striking down rights violations. Argentina lacked constitutional human rights catalog until 1994 reform, and even post-reform, judicial deference to executive on emergencies remains high.

**3. Parliamentary Oversight**: *Emergencies Act* Section 58 creates Parliamentary Review Committee (House + Senate members) that monitors emergency measures in real time and reports to Parliament. Prime Minister must table declaration before Parliament within 7 days; if Parliament is not sitting, it is recalled. This ensures legislative scrutiny during emergency, not just ex post. Argentina's Congress typically delegates emergency powers to President without ongoing oversight.

**4. Mandatory Public Inquiry**: Section 63 requires a public inquiry into emergency's circumstances and handling within 60 days of expiration. Rouleau Commission (2022-2023) held 6 weeks of hearings, reviewed 9,000+ documents, and published 2,000-page report criticizing aspects of government response. This creates **reputational costs** for misuse and political incentives for restraint. Argentina has no such requirement: emergency measures are rarely evaluated retrospectively.

**5. Federal-Provincial Balance**: Canada's federalism gives provinces primary responsibility for "property and civil rights" (Constitution Act 1867, Section 92). Federal *Emergencies Act* can be invoked only when situation "exceeds the capacity or authority of a province." This creates threshold requirement: federal government must demonstrate provincial inadequacy before intervening, limiting federal expansion. Argentina's federal system lacks clear competency boundaries, enabling federal encroachment.

**Result**: Canada demonstrates that **vague language + procedural friction + accountability = low mutation rate**. The *Emergencies Act* grants broad discretion ("threats to security of Canada"), but sunset clauses, judicial review, parliamentary oversight, and mandatory inquiry prevent permanent expansion.

#### Comparative Lessons for Constitutional Design

The Swiss and Canadian experiences suggest **institutional design principles** for preventing mutation of emergency powers:

| Design Feature | Switzerland | Canada | Argentina | Effect on Mutation |
|----------------|-------------|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Automatic sunset** | Yes (referendum if >1 year) | Yes (30-120 days) | No | Strong prevention |
| **Judicial review** | Yes (proportionality) | Yes (Charter) | Weak (1990-2003) | Moderate prevention |
| **Federalism** | Strong (cantonal veto) | Moderate (provincial primacy) | Weak (fiscal dependence) | Strong prevention |
| **Direct democracy** | Yes (mandatory referendum) | No | No | Strong prevention |
| **Parliamentary oversight** | Yes (both chambers + cantonal) | Yes (Review Committee) | Minimal | Moderate prevention |
| **Ex post accountability** | Yes (reports to Parliament) | Yes (mandatory inquiry) | No | Moderate prevention |

The table reveals a **cumulative effect**: Switzerland and Canada have multiple overlapping checks, creating redundancy. Even if one check fails (e.g., parliamentary oversight weak), others compensate (e.g., judicial review or sunset). Argentina lacked multiple checks simultaneously (weak federalism + weak judiciary + no sunset + no oversight), creating perfect conditions for mutation.

**Key theoretical insight**: **Vague language is necessary but not sufficient for mutation**. Argentina's "bienestar general" created linguistic vulnerability, but mutation occurred because institutional checks were absent. Switzerland and Canada have comparable vagueness but resist mutation through friction. This suggests constitutional design should focus less on eliminating vague language (often impossible—emergencies are by nature unpredictable) and more on **multiplying checks** that impose costs on mutation.

**Mutation resistance equation**:
```
Mutation Probability = f(Vague Language) × g(Institutional Friction)^-1
```
Where:
- High vague language + Low friction = High mutation (Argentina)
- High vague language + High friction = Low mutation (Switzerland, Canada)
- Low vague language + Low friction = Low mutation (rare; most constitutions have some vague terms)

This equation explains why efforts to write "perfectly clear" constitutions often fail: achieving zero vagueness is impossible (language is inherently indeterminate), and even precise language can be reinterpreted creatively. More effective strategy is to accept vagueness and **maximize friction** through procedural safeguards.
